# STT & TTS User Survey Evaluation Methodology

This document outlines the structured framework for recruiting users and conducting evaluations of Speech-to-Text (STT) transcription accuracy and Text-to-Speech (TTS) voice synthesis quality.

---

## 1. Recruitment Strategy & Cohort Design

To obtain valid evaluations for local speech models, you should recruit a panel of **5 to 10 human evaluators**. 

### 👥 Cohort Composition
Aim for diversity in your evaluator group to ensure generalizability:
- **Language Profile**: 60-70% Native English speakers, 30-40% Non-native English speakers (to evaluate how models perform under different accent comprehension scenarios).
- **Age Distribution**: A range across 18–60 years (different age groups have varying sensitivities to voice frequency range and speech speed).
- **Listening Devices**: Require evaluators to use headphones/earphones (preferred) or a quiet room environment.

### ✉️ Sample Recruitment Template
```text
Subject: Invite: 10-Minute Local Speech Technology Evaluation Survey

Hi [Name],

We are benchmarking our new local speech services (Speech-to-Text and Text-to-Speech) and would love your feedback! 

We need 5–10 people to test our models by listening to voice notes, reading transcriptions, and scoring them on a simple 1 to 5 scale. 

- Time commitment: ~10 minutes
- Requirements: Headphones and a quiet room
- Link to survey: http://localhost:8000

Your feedback will directly influence which voice synthesis and transcription models we select for our production platform.

Best regards,
[Your Name/Team]
```

## 2. Evaluation Protocol & Survey Design

The survey is divided into two sequential segments: Text-to-Speech (TTS) Voice Quality and Speech-to-Text (STT) Transcription Quality.

### A. Text-to-Speech (TTS) Quality Evaluation
Evaluators will listen to audio clips generated by **5 Piper voice models**:
1. *Amy (Medium)*
2. *Lessac (Medium)*
3. *Joe (Medium)*
4. *Ryan (Medium)*
5. *Danny (Low)*

For each audio sample, users will rate the following dimensions on a 1–5 scale:
- **Naturalness**: Does the voice flow naturally like a human, or does it sound robotic/choppy?
- **Pronunciation**: Are the words spoken correctly, particularly multi-syllable terms?
- **Intonation**: Is the vocal pitch, emphasis, and emotional cadence appropriate for the context?
- **Overall Quality**: The evaluator's overall perception of the voice.

### B. Reference Audio Quality Evaluation
Evaluators will listen to reference recordings (`data/audio/*.wav`) and evaluate the audio quality based on the following dimensions:
- **Speech Clarity**: How clearly are the spoken words articulated in the recording?
- **Word Intelligibility**: How easy is it to understand the sentence structure and vocabulary?
- **Background Noise Control**: Is there distracting background noise, clipping, or hum?
- **Overall Audio Quality**: The evaluator's overall rating of the recorded audio quality.

## 3. Mean Opinion Score (MOS) Scoring Rubric

The Mean Opinion Score (MOS) is a standard metric in audio and telecommunication engineering. The scoring is mapped using these explicit quality anchors:

| Score | Quality Rating | TTS Description (Voice Quality) | Audio Quality Description (Reference Audio) |
| :---: | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **5** | **Excellent** | Completely natural. Perfect human-like cadence, phrasing, and timing. No artifacts. | Crystal clear audio. No background noise, distortion, or clipping. Perfectly intelligible. |
| **4** | **Good** | Highly intelligible and mostly natural. Rare, minor robotic inflections. | Clear audio with very minor background hum or echo that does not distract. |
| **3** | **Fair** | Understandable, but clearly synthetic. Noticeable robotic tone or static background artifacts. | Intelligible, but contains noticeable noise, static, or echo. Requires slightly more listening effort. |
| **2** | **Poor** | Difficult to understand. Robotic, heavily distorted, or has severe timing/choppiness. | Low clarity. Distorted audio, significant background noise, or heavy clipping making words difficult to hear. |
| **1** | **Bad** | Unintelligible. Complete distortion, wrong speed, or extremely robotic noise. | Completely unintelligible. Extreme distortion, cutouts, or loud static noise rendering audio useless. |

## 4. Analytical Framework

Once responses are collected, the backend calculates the following statistical metrics for each model:

### 1. Mean Opinion Score (MOS)
The average score for model $m$:
$$\text{MOS}_m = \bar{X}_m = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_{i,m}$$
Where:
- $N$ is the total number of evaluations/samples rated for model $m$.
- $X_{i,m}$ is the score (1–5) given in evaluation $i$.

### 2. Sample Standard Deviation ($S_m$)
Measures the variance/disagreement among evaluators:
$$S_m = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (X_{i,m} - \bar{X}_m)^2}$$

### 3. Margin of Error & 95% Confidence Interval (CI)
Indicates the range within which the true population mean lies with 95% confidence:
$$\text{CI}_m = \bar{X}_m \pm z \cdot \left(\frac{S_m}{\sqrt{N}}\right)$$
Where:
- $z = 1.96$ (critical value for a 95% confidence level under normal distribution).
- $\frac{S_m}{\sqrt{N}}$ is the Standard Error of the Mean (SEM).

*Note: For smaller sample sizes ($N < 30$), the Student's t-distribution with $N - 1$ degrees of freedom is technically preferred. However, for a quick and standardized UI comparison, the $z = 1.96$ formulation provides a reliable visual error bar.*
